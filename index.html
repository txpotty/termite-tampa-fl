Advanced Treatment Methods: Targeting Termites Where They Live

We utilize a range of advanced and proven treatment methods to effectively eliminate termites. The chosen method (or combination of methods) will depend on the specific situation. Some of the techniques we may employ include:

Liquid Termiticide Barrier Treatments:

Applying liquid termiticide to the soil around the foundation of your property creates a continuous barrier that repels and kills termites attempting to enter. This is a highly effective method for preventing subterranean termites from accessing your building.

Termite Baiting Systems:

Baiting systems involve strategically placing stations containing a slow-acting termiticide around your property. Termites feed on the bait and carry it back to their colony, eventually eliminating the entire colony. This is an environmentally friendly and effective option, especially for ongoing monitoring and control.

Direct Wood Treatments:

For localized infestations or areas of damaged wood, we may apply direct wood treatments to eliminate termites present within the structure itself. These treatments can penetrate the wood and provide long-lasting protection.

Common Termite Problems and How We Solve Them

Recognizing the signs of a termite infestation is the first step towards protecting your property in the Tampa area. Termites are often called ‘silent destroyers’ because they can cause significant damage unnoticed for long periods. Knowing what to look for can help you identify a problem early, allowing for prompt and effective intervention. Ignoring these signs can lead to extensive and costly structural damage over time.

Mud Tubes: A Clear Sign of Subterranean Termites

Mud tubes, often resembling narrow tunnels made of soil and wood particles, are a strong indicator of subterranean termite activity. These tubes serve as protected pathways for termites as they travel from their underground colony to your property to access food sources (wood). Finding mud tubes on your foundation, walls, or other structures means termites are actively infesting your property.

Our service addresses mud tubes by first identifying the type of termite creating them through our comprehensive inspection. We then implement a targeted treatment plan, often involving liquid termiticides applied to the soil around your foundation to create a barrier that termites cannot cross. This treatment not only eliminates the termites currently using the tubes but also prevents new termites from accessing your property from the ground. We also recommend removing any wood-to-soil contact points around your property, as these are prime areas for termites to establish mud tubes and begin infesting your structure.

Damaged Wood: The Result of Termite Feeding

Termites feed on cellulose found in wood, and their feeding activity can lead to visibly damaged wood. This damage might appear as hollowed-out sections, blistering paint, or wood that sounds hollow when tapped. In advanced infestations, you may even see tunnels or galleries within the wood itself. This kind of damage indicates an active and potentially widespread infestation that requires immediate attention to prevent further structural compromise.

If we discover damaged wood during our inspection, it helps us pinpoint areas of active infestation and the extent of the problem. Our treatment plan will include measures to eliminate the termites within the damaged wood, such as direct wood treatments or the use of baiting systems strategically placed near infested areas. Addressing the source of the infestation and eliminating the colony is crucial to stop the feeding and prevent future damage to other wooden elements of your property. We also offer recommendations for repairing or replacing badly damaged wood to restore structural integrity and aesthetics.

Discarded Termite Wings: Evidence of a Swarm

Termite swarmers, reproductive termites that emerge from mature colonies to mate and establish new colonies, shed their wings shortly after swarming. Finding piles of discarded wings, often near windowsills, doorways, or other light sources, is a definitive sign of recent termite activity, specifically swarming. This indicates that a mature colony is present nearby and that new colonies may be forming.

Discovering discarded wings prompts us to conduct a thorough investigation to locate the source of the swarm and identify the location of the parent colony. While swarming itself isn’t a direct threat to your property at that moment, it signifies the presence of a mature infestation that is actively reproducing. Our treatment will focus on eliminating the existing colony through methods like barrier treatments or baiting systems to prevent further swarming and the establishment of new colonies on or near your property. We also educate our clients on what to look for during termite swarming season in Tampa to help them identify potential issues early.

Live Termite Sightings: Direct Confirmation of Infestation

Seeing live termites, particularly in or around your home or business, is a clear and undeniable sign of an active infestation. This might include seeing swarmers during swarming season, worker termites within damaged wood, or even termites in other areas of your property. Seeing live termites indicates an active and potentially significant problem that requires immediate professional intervention.

A sighting of live termites allows us to quickly focus our inspection and treatment efforts on the areas where activity has been observed. We will confirm the type of termite and implement a targeted treatment plan to eliminate the visible termites and, more importantly, the hidden colony from which they originated. Addressing live sightings promptly is essential to prevent the infestation from spreading and causing more extensive damage to your property. We use effective and fast-acting methods to eliminate live termites while also implementing long-term control strategies.

Subtle Structural Changes: Hidden Termite Activity

Termite damage can sometimes manifest as subtle structural changes that homeowners or business owners might not immediately attribute to pests. This can include floors that are buckling or sagging, ceilings that are bulging, doors or windows that are difficult to open or close due to warped frames, or even a faint, musty odor resembling mildew. These changes indicate that termites have been actively feeding within the structure, potentially causing significant internal damage that is not immediately visible from the exterior.

Our detailed inspection process is designed to identify these subtle signs of hidden termite activity that might be overlooked by the untrained eye. We utilize specialized tools and techniques to assess the structural integrity of your property and confirm if termites are the cause of these changes. Once we identify hidden infestations, our treatment plan will focus on reaching the termites within the structure, often using direct wood treatments or injected termiticides, in addition to barrier or baiting systems to address the source of the infestation. Addressing these hidden problems before they become major structural failures is critical to preserving the value and safety of your property.

Termite Droppings (Frass): Another Clue for Drywood Termites

Drywood termites, which infest wood directly (unlike subterranean termites that live in soil), leave behind tiny, pellet-like droppings called frass. Finding small piles of frass, often near wooden structures or even outside small kick-out holes in the wood surface, is a strong indication of a drywood termite infestation. The color of the frass can sometimes even indicate the type of wood being infested.

If we find frass during our inspection, it directs our attention to potential drywood termite activity. Treatment for drywood termites differs from that for subterranean termites and may involve localized spot treatments, fumigation (in severe, widespread infestations), or heat treatments. We will tailor our approach based on the extent and location of the drywood termite infestation, focusing on eliminating the termites within the infested wood and preventing further infestations. Our expertise in identifying different types of termites and their specific behaviors is essential for implementing the most effective treatment strategy. We also provide recommendations for sealing potential entry points to prevent future drywood termite infestations.

Pest Identification: Knowing What You’re Dealing With

Sometimes, you might see insects that you suspect are termites but aren’t entirely sure. Identifying the specific type of pest is crucial for effective treatment. Termites are often mistaken for ants, especially during swarming season, but there are key differences in their appearance (straight antennae, uniform waist for termites vs. elbowed antennae, pinched waist for ants)
